Build No. 01 · Career Agent

The Job Search Agent that actually reads.

An always-on agent that reads job alerts in your Gmail, scores each role against a rubric you write once, pushes the top picks to your phone via iMessage twice a week, and learns from your feedback so it gets sharper every run. I built it for my wife. Worked example here: a junior dev landing their first role in Berlin.

By Erion Maxhari 30-min setup Free if you have Claude + Gmail + Notion

01What it does

You sign up for job alerts on LinkedIn, StepStone, Indeed, Xing — the usual suspects. They flood your inbox. You skim a handful, file the rest under "I'll look later," and never look later.

This agent intercepts those alerts. It reads each job description, scores it against a rubric you wrote in plain language ("score higher if commute is under 40 minutes, lower if it requires 5+ years experience"), and twice a week sends a ranked digest — to a Notion page for the full version, and to iMessage as a push notification with the top 3:

Job digest · Mon morning Mon 04 May
Junior Frontend Developer · Solarisbank 87 / 100
Berlin-Mitte · 4 km · €48–55k · Hybrid 2 days
Strong match: React + TS stack, explicit junior-friendly language ("first or second role"), commute under your 40-min cap. Mentorship program advertised.
Web Developer · Zalando 62 / 100
Berlin-Friedrichshain · 9 km · Salary not stated · Hybrid
Mid-level role but description says "open to strong juniors with portfolio." Tech stack matches. Worth applying with a tailored cover letter.
Senior React Engineer · N26 28 / 100
Berlin-Mitte · 6 km · €75–95k · Hybrid
Filtered out: 5+ years required, "senior" level. Skip unless you want a stretch application for visibility.

Then — and this is what makes it actually useful — you reply with what you thought. "This one looked great but the team is all senior, I'd be alone." "This one I applied to." "This one was recruiter spam." The agent reads your feedback before the next run and adjusts. The rubric becomes a living thing instead of a frozen guess.

02The stack

Four pieces. All free or cheap:

  • Gmail — where the alerts arrive. A label routes them to the agent.
  • Claude with Cowork — the brain. Cowork is the part that lets the prompt run unattended on a schedule. Without it you'd be pasting the prompt manually every Monday.
  • Notion — two pages. One for the digest. One for your feedback. Free.
  • iMessage — push notification with the top picks. The reason you actually read the digest. Cowork can send messages to a phone number you've verified.

If you're on Android, swap iMessage for Telegram or WhatsApp Web. The principle is the same: deliver the digest where you already look, not where you have to remember to go.

03Setup, end to end

Roughly 30 minutes if you've used Claude before, 45 if you haven't. Don't skip step 4 — the rubric is the whole thing.

  1. Set up a Gmail label and filter. Create a label called Jobs/Alerts. Then create a filter: From: linkedin.com OR stepstone.de OR indeed.de OR xing.com → apply label, mark as read. Sign up for daily digests on each platform with your real keywords. Subscribe broadly — let the agent filter, not the platform.
  2. Create two Notion pages. Job Digest — the agent appends a dated section here every run. Job Feedback — empty for now. You write here after every digest. Free text. "Applied to Solarisbank." "Zalando looked good but they want 3 years exp." "Skip anything from recruiter X." This is the loop.
  3. Connect Claude to Gmail and Notion. Settings → Connectors. Enable Gmail and Notion. Authorise both. You'll need a paid plan for Cowork scheduled tasks — check the pricing page.
  4. Write your scoring rubric. See section 4. Write it once, save it as a Notion page you can paste from. Expect to rewrite it twice in the first month — that's normal.
  5. Build the Cowork task. Cowork → Schedule → New Task. Name it Job Digest. Frequency: every Monday and Thursday at 8 AM (see section 7 on why twice a week). Paste this prompt, replacing the rubric block with your own: 
    You are my job-search agent. You run twice a week.

WHAT TO READ:
1. Search Gmail for emails labelled "Jobs/Alerts" from the last 4 days.
2. Read every distinct job listing inside (deduplicate cross-postings).
3. ALSO read the Notion page titled "Job Feedback" — every entry the
   user has written since last run. Use this to adjust scoring.

WHO I AM AND WHAT I WANT:
[Paste your full rubric here — see section 4]

WHAT TO DO:
1. Score each job 0-100 using the rubric, modified by the latest feedback.
2. Sort highest to lowest.
3. For each job 40+, write a one-paragraph "why" — the fit AND the
   caveats, in plain language.
4. Group: Strong matches (70+), Worth applying (50-69), Stretch / skip
   (40-49). Drop anything below 40.
5. Append the result as a new dated section to the Notion page
   "Job Digest".
6. Send an iMessage to [your number]: top 3 only, with score and a
   one-line reason each. End with: "Full digest in Notion."
7. At the end of the Notion digest, add: "Feedback for next run? Add a
   note to the Job Feedback page."

TONE:
Direct. No filler. If a role is bad, say so in one sentence.
Don't write cover letters. Don't apply. Don't summarise — rank.
  6. Run it manually first. Don't wait for Monday. Trigger the task once, watch it work, read the output. The first run will be slightly wrong. Fix the rubric before you let it loose on the schedule.
  7. Verify your phone for iMessage delivery. In Cowork, add the phone number that should receive the digest. You'll get a one-time code to confirm. Pin the iMessage thread on your phone. From now on the digest arrives as a notification on Mon/Thu morning.
  8. Write your first feedback entry. After the first real digest, open Job Feedback and write three lines. "Applied to A. Skipped B because senior team. C looked spammy." This trains you to use the loop. The agent reads this on the next run.

04The rubric — the hard part

The rubric is the whole game. A bad rubric scores everything 70 and the digest is noise. A good rubric ranks ten jobs and the top three are obvious applies.

Three rules

One: weights must add to 100. Forces you to choose what matters most. If "junior-friendly framing" is 35 points and "salary" is 10, you've made a real decision.

Two: every criterion must be observable in a job description. "Good company culture" is unscoreable. "Mentions mentorship, training program, or pair programming" is scoreable.

Three: include at least one disqualifier. A criterion that, if missing, caps the score. For a junior, "requires 5+ years experience" should hard-cap at 30. For someone with kids, "fully on-site, no remote at all" might cap.

The template

WHO I AM
[2-3 sentences. Role, location, level, what you're optimising for.]

DEAL-BREAKERS (auto-cap at 30)
- [Thing that makes a job impossible for you]
- [Another one]

SCORING (must total 100)
- [Criterion 1]                                    (X points)
- [Criterion 2]                                    (X points)
- [Criterion 3]                                    (X points)
- [Criterion 4]                                    (X points)
- [Criterion 5]                                    (X points)

BONUS POINTS (up to +10, can push past 100)
- [Things that delight you when you see them]

NEVER SCORE ABOVE 50 IF
- [Conditions that signal a poor fit even if other criteria match]

05Worked example: Alex, junior dev, Berlin

Meet our hypothetical user. Alex, 24, finished a 9-month bootcamp last autumn. Knows React, TypeScript, basic Node. No professional experience yet, two portfolio projects on GitHub. Lives in Neukölln, doesn't own a car, wants to be in an office at least 3 days a week ("I'll go mad alone in my room"). Berlin only.

Here's Alex's filled-in rubric:

WHO I AM
Junior frontend dev, Berlin-Neukölln, looking for first
professional role. Bootcamp graduate, two portfolio projects
shipped. Optimising for: learning environment, in-office
collaboration, manageable commute. Not optimising for: salary.

DEAL-BREAKERS (auto-cap at 30)
- Requires 3+ years professional experience
- 100% remote (I want office time)
- Outside Berlin, no relocation help

SCORING (totals 100)
- Junior-friendly framing                          35
  ("entry-level", "first role", "junior", "graduate", or
  explicit mention of bootcamp grads being welcome)

- Tech stack match                                 25
  Full match (React + TS): 25
  Partial (one of them, or Vue/Angular): 15
  Backend-heavy or unrelated: 0

- Commute from Neukölln under 40 min by transit    20
  Under 25 min: 20
  25-40 min: 12
  40-60 min: 5
  Over 60 min: 0

- In-office or hybrid (2+ days/week)               10
  Hybrid 3+ days: 10
  Hybrid 2 days: 7
  Hybrid 1 day: 3
  Fully remote: 0

- Mentorship, training, or junior program          10
  Explicit program: 10
  "We support juniors / pair programming": 5
  Nothing mentioned: 0

BONUS (up to +10)
- Visa sponsorship advertised: +5
- German not required: +5
- Public benefit / climate / education company: +3

NEVER SCORE ABOVE 50 IF
- Description is vague and lists no concrete tech stack
- Company has fewer than 10 employees (early-stage risk for a
  first role; Alex needs structure)

Translating it for your own situation

  • "Who I am" — write the version that's actually true. Two sentences. If you can't write it, you don't know what you want yet.
  • "Deal-breakers" — what would make you turn down a job in the first 30 seconds of reading? Those.
  • "Scoring" — five criteria max. If you have ten, you have noise. Force yourself to choose.

06Delivery: Notion + iMessage

Where the digest lands matters as much as how good it is. If you have to remember to open it, you won't.

iMessage — the short version

Top 3 only. Role, company, score, one-line reason. Arrives as a notification on Mon/Thu morning. You read it in 20 seconds with coffee. If something looks promising, you tap into Notion for the full reasoning. If nothing does, you go on with your day.

Cowork sends the message to a phone number you've verified on your account. Use your own number, or share a thread with a partner if you're job hunting together. Don't deliver to your work Slack or your main email — those are full of noise, and the whole point of the digest is to be the *only* job-search signal you see twice a week.

Notion — the long version

The full digest with all jobs, scores, and reasoning. Append-only — every run becomes a new dated section, so you can scroll back and see what scored well two weeks ago. This is also where you write your feedback (separate page, not the digest itself).

Cross-platform note

iMessage is Apple-only. On Android, the same pattern works with Telegram (free, has bots), WhatsApp Web (cleaner UI), or even SMS via Twilio (~€0.05/message in Germany). The key is the delivery channel you actually look at, not the technology behind it.

07Cowork scheduling: daily or twice a week?

People ask if it should run every day. Don't.

Twice a week is right for most people. Mon morning catches the weekend's batch. Thu morning catches early-week postings while there's still time to apply before Friday afternoon. Two notifications, both actionable.

Daily creates fatigue. Job alerts don't move that fast. Most postings stay open for 2–4 weeks. A daily digest starts feeling like spam by week two, and you stop opening it. Once the digest becomes ignorable, the loop is dead.

Once a week is fine for senior roles. If you're 8+ years in, the role density is lower and you're not in a rush — Monday morning only is plenty.

If you genuinely have a deadline ("I need to leave my current job in 60 days"), bump to three times a week — Mon, Wed, Fri. Beyond that, the agent isn't your bottleneck, your application throughput is.

Adjusting frequency mid-search You can change the schedule any time in Cowork. I usually recommend starting with twice weekly, dropping to once if you're getting too many results, bumping to three if you're getting too few. The rubric should be the dial you turn most, not the schedule.

08The feedback loop — how it gets smarter

This is what separates a useful agent from a glorified email filter. The rubric you write in week one will be wrong — not catastrophically, but wrong in ways you only see once you've read 30 scored jobs. The feedback loop fixes the rubric for you, in plain language, without forcing you to rewrite it.

How it works

Every run, the agent reads your feedback page before scoring. New entries since last run get treated as additions to the rubric. You write:

Mon 27 Apr — Skipped the Solarisbank one even though it scored 87.
Reason: read the JD again, the team is 6 seniors. I'd be the only
junior. Score that lower next time. Add a criterion: ratio of
junior-to-senior on the team if mentioned.

Mon 27 Apr — Applied to Zalando. Tailored cover letter, took an
hour. Worth doing again for similar matches.

Thu 30 Apr — That recruiter agency "TechHire" sent the same role
under three different titles. Filter their domain at Gmail level,
not here.

And the agent, on the next run, will:

  • Score down jobs where the team composition signals lone-junior risk
  • Treat your "applied" notes as confirmation that high-scoring matches are working — not change anything
  • Flag the recruiter issue for you to fix at Gmail filter level (not pretend it can fix it itself)

What feedback is good for

  • Correcting the rubric — "I keep skipping anything that says fast-paced" → add it as a soft cap.
  • Tracking what you applied to — informal, just a line. Notion is fine for this.
  • Calibration drift — "everything is scoring 60–80" → ask the agent to spread scores more aggressively.
  • Things the agent can't see — interview vibes, recruiter behaviour, Glassdoor reviews you read.

What feedback is NOT good for

  • Long structured forms. Free text. Three lines per entry max. The moment it feels like work, you stop, and the loop dies.
  • Detailed application tracking. If you want a real CRM, use a separate Notion database with columns. The feedback page is for *adjusting the agent*, not project-managing your search.
  • Asking the agent to write your cover letter. Don't. See section 11.
The unfair advantage After three weeks, your agent's scoring will be sharper than any job board's "recommended for you" engine — because it has feedback from one specific person (you), not aggregate behaviour from millions. That's the whole reason this is worth building.

09Common mistakes

"Everything scores 70."

Your rubric is too generous. Look at the weights — are three different criteria all worth 30+ points? You're rewarding presence, not selectivity. Tighten one criterion to be binary, and add a "never above 50 if" clause.

"Nothing scores above 40."

Too many deal-breakers, or they're too vague. "Bad company culture" can't be a deal-breaker — the agent can't see culture in a JD. Replace with something observable like "team size under 10."

"The top result is always one I'd never apply to."

The rubric rewards the wrong things. Look at the top match — what scored high that shouldn't have? That's your missing "never above 50 if." Add it. Try again Monday.

"I forget to write feedback."

The digest itself reminds you (the prompt has the line "Feedback for next run?"). If you still forget, the issue isn't memory — it's that you're not actually job hunting. The agent runs forever; the loop only matters if you're applying.

"The agent is summarising instead of ranking."

You wrote a soft prompt. Add explicit language: "Don't summarise. Don't be balanced. Sort, score, cut." Be rude to the agent — it works.

"It surfaces the same jobs every run."

Cross-posting on multiple platforms means deduplication is failing. Add to the prompt: "Deduplicate by company + role title + city. If a posting appeared in a previous digest (check the Notion Job Digest page for the last 14 days), skip it unless the score has changed by more than 10 points."

10Variations for different situations

Same template, different weights. Don't copy these blindly — translate them to your own facts.

Senior with kids

Salary becomes a foundation, not a tiebreaker. Weight it 25–30. Mentorship goes to zero (you're the mentor now). Add "school-run-friendly hours" or "core hours flexible" as a 15-point criterion. Remote-friendly becomes a positive (+10), not a negative. Deal-breaker: anything labelled "fast-paced startup" or "wear many hats" — code for unsustainable hours.

Career switcher into tech (no professional dev experience)

You're Alex's harder-mode cousin. Weight "junior-friendly framing" up to 40. Add a hard "never above 50 if" for anything requiring a CS degree. Drop tech-stack-match if your stack is broad — companies care about your trajectory, not your toolbelt. Add bonus points for "we hire from non-traditional backgrounds" language.

Visa-needed, EU-wide

Geography opens up but visa sponsorship becomes the gatekeeper. Make "explicit visa sponsorship advertised" a 30-point criterion, not a +5 bonus. Hard-cap at 20 if visa sponsorship is unmentioned (most places will silently reject sponsorship requests). Drop commute entirely; replace with "company has demonstrated history of hiring non-EU candidates" — observable through Glassdoor and LinkedIn employee profiles.

Remote-first, EU-wide

Commute drops to zero. Time-zone overlap becomes the new commute (15 points: "Berlin time zone ±2 hours"). Salary band transparency becomes a soft cap — most remote companies post salary bands; the ones that don't are usually paying less than you'd accept. Deal-breaker: companies requiring "occasional travel to HQ" if HQ is a 10-hour flight away — they'll quietly start expecting it monthly.

11What this won't do — on purpose

  • It won't apply for you. Applying is a decision, not a task. The friction is also the filter that stops you from spamming.
  • It won't write your cover letter. Don't. AI-generated cover letters are already what recruiters complain about most — the signal is dead. The cover letter is where the human makes the case for the human. Outsource the filtering. Never outsource the asking.
  • It won't fine-tune your CV per job. Same reason. Tailor your CV by hand for the top 2–3 jobs you actually want. The rest of the time, your generic CV is fine.
  • It won't find jobs that aren't in your inbox. Garbage alerts in, garbage digest out. Subscribe broadly to good sources.
  • It won't replace networking. The best jobs are filled before they're posted. The agent helps with the ~70% that go through job boards.
  • It won't keep you motivated. A digest doesn't apply itself. If your problem is "I have 12 high-scoring matches and haven't applied to any," the agent isn't the issue.

12What's next

The Job Search Agent is one piece of a larger system. The full job-landing pipeline has four stages, and the agent only owns the first one. Future builds in this journal will cover the rest:

Stage 1 · This build

Filter the noise

What you just read. Get from 200 alerts a week down to 5 ranked picks delivered to your phone.

Stage 2 · Coming next

CV review agent

Paste a job description and your CV. The agent critiques the CV against that specific role — gaps, weak claims, missing keywords for ATS — without rewriting it for you. Use it on your top 2–3 applications, not all of them.

Stage 3 · Later

Interview prep agent

Same input (JD + CV). Agent generates the 8–10 questions most likely to come up, grills you on each one, and gives feedback on your answers. Especially useful for technical screens and behavioural rounds you can rehearse.

Stage 4 · Later

Application tracker

Notion database template, not an app. Columns for company, role, status, next action, follow-up date. Boring on purpose. The point is to never lose track of where you applied and when to follow up — most candidates lose interviews to silence, not rejection.

Each one stands alone. Each one solves one specific problem. Together they cover the journey from "noisy inbox" to "signed offer." If you want me to ping you when the next one ships, email me below — I'll keep a small list, no newsletter, just a heads-up when there's something new.
